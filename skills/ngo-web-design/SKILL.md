# NGO Web Design & Planning

This skill guides the full planning and design process for websites serving political NGOs,
associations, and civil society organizations. The process is fundamentally different from
commercial web design because the goals are different: you're not optimizing for conversion
or revenue — you're building for trust, transparency, mobilization, and democratic participation.

---

## Why NGO Web Design is Different

Commercial web design optimizes for: purchase → retention → upsell.
NGO web design optimizes for: trust → understanding → action → belonging.

This changes everything:

| Commercial Site | NGO / Association Site |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Hero: product benefit | Hero: mission and urgency |
| CTA: "Buy Now" | CTA: "Join Us" / "Take Action" / "Donate" |
| Social proof: customer reviews | Social proof: impact numbers, endorsements, media coverage |
| About: founding story | About: governance, transparency, who we are and why |
| Pricing page | Donation tiers / membership structure |
| Blog: SEO content | Blog: position papers, campaign updates, political education |
| Footer: legal minimum | Footer: full transparency (Impressum, Satzung, Rechenschaftsbericht) |

The user visiting an NGO site is asking: "Can I trust these people? Do they share my values?
What have they actually achieved? How can I help?" Every design decision should answer these
questions.

## The NGO Planning Process

Don't jump into design. NGO websites fail when they're treated as brochures. Follow this
process:

### Phase 1: Mission Alignment

Before any wireframes, answer these questions:

**Identity**
- What is the organization's legal form? (e.V., gGmbH, Stiftung, informal group, party-adjacent)
- What is the core mission in one sentence?
- What are the 2-3 key campaigns or programs right now?
- Who are the people behind it? (Board, team, volunteers — transparency matters)

**Audiences** (NGOs always have multiple, and they need different things)
- **Supporters / potential members**: Need to understand the mission and feel motivated to join
- **Existing members**: Need updates, internal documents, event info, participation tools
- **Media / journalists**: Need press releases, positions, spokesperson contacts, high-res logos
- **Politicians / institutions**: Need policy positions, data, credibility signals
- **Funders / donors**: Need impact reports, financial transparency, donation mechanisms
- **General public**: Need accessible explainers of what the org does and why it matters

**Action hierarchy** — What do you most want a visitor to do?
Rank these for the specific organization:
1. Donate
2. Become a member
3. Sign a petition / take action
4. Attend an event
5. Share / amplify
6. Contact for press or partnership
7. Learn / educate themselves

This hierarchy drives the information architecture and CTA placement.

### Phase 2: Legal and Compliance Requirements

NGO websites in Germany have specific legal obligations. Get these right from the start —
retrofitting is painful and non-compliance can result in Abmahnungen.

**Mandatory for every site:**

- **Impressum** (§5 TMG / §18 MStV): Must be reachable from every page in max 2 clicks.
  Must include: full legal name, legal form, registered address, board members (Vorstand),
  Vereinsregister number and court, contact email and phone, responsible person for content
  (V.i.S.d.P. if publishing editorial content). For a political association, also include
  the Steuernummer if receiving donations with Zuwendungsbescheinigungen.

- **Datenschutzerklärung** (DSGVO/GDPR): Privacy policy covering all data processing — contact
  forms, newsletter signup, donation processing, analytics, embedded content (YouTube, social
  media). Must name the Datenschutzbeauftragter if the org has one (required if >20 people
  regularly process personal data).

- **Cookie consent**: Must obtain consent before any non-essential cookies or tracking. Analytics
  (even privacy-friendly ones like Matomo/Plausible) need consent unless configured to be
  fully anonymized. Embedded YouTube/Twitter/social widgets count as third-party cookies.

- **Accessibility statement** (BITV 2.0 / BFSG): Not legally mandatory for all NGOs yet, but
  strongly recommended — especially if the org receives public funding or works in the political
  space. Demonstrates commitment to inclusion. WCAG 2.1 AA is the baseline standard.

**Specific to associations and political NGOs:**

- **Satzung** (bylaws): Should be publicly accessible on the website. Transparency builds trust
  and is expected by potential members and funders.

- **Rechenschaftsbericht / Jahresbericht**: Annual report or financial transparency. Major funders
  and institutions expect this. Consider a dedicated "Transparenz" page.

- **Zuwendungsbescheinigungen**: If the org can issue tax-deductible donation receipts
  (gemeinnützig status), state this clearly near donation CTAs with the Finanzamt confirmation.

- **Political activity boundaries**: If the org has Gemeinnützigkeit status, be careful about
  how campaign language is framed on the website. Direct partisan campaigning can endanger
  tax-exempt status. Political education and issue advocacy are generally fine; direct support
  for parties or candidates is not. When in doubt, frame as "politische Bildung" not
  "Wahlkampf."

### Phase 3: Information Architecture

NGO sites need a different structure than commercial sites. Here's a starting architecture
that works for most political associations — adapt it, don't copy it blindly.

**Primary navigation:**

```
[Logo + Org Name]

├── Über uns          → Mission, team, governance, history, Satzung
├── Themen / Arbeit   → Issue areas, campaigns, position papers
├── Mitmachen         → Join, volunteer, events, action tools
├── Spenden           → Donation page with tiers and transparency
├── Aktuelles         → News, press releases, blog, campaign updates
└── Kontakt / Presse  → Contact form, press kit, spokesperson info
```

**Footer (full transparency zone):**

```
├── Impressum
├── Datenschutz
├── Barrierefreiheit (accessibility statement)
├── Satzung (PDF or page)
├── Jahresbericht / Transparenz
├── Newsletter signup
└── Social media links
```

**Key principle: Don't hide governance.** Commercial sites bury legal pages. NGO sites should
make transparency a feature, not a footnote. A visitor who can find your bylaws, your board,
and your finances in 10 seconds trusts you more than one who finds a slick hero video.

### Phase 4: Content Strategy

NGO content serves different purposes than commercial content:

**Mission content** (permanent, rarely changes)
- Who we are, what we stand for, our theory of change
- Team and board with photos and brief bios (humanizes the org)
- History and milestones (builds credibility)

**Campaign content** (time-bound, urgency-driven)
- Current campaigns with clear asks: sign this, show up here, share this
- Campaign landing pages optimized for a single action
- Sharable assets: social media graphics, key facts, talking points

**Education content** (evergreen, SEO-relevant)
- Explainers on the issues the org works on
- Position papers and policy briefs
- FAQ pages that anticipate public questions

**Accountability content** (periodic, trust-building)
- Annual reports, impact metrics
- Financial transparency
- How donations are used (ideally with specific examples)

**Member content** (gated or semi-gated)
- Event calendar, internal updates
- Member assembly (Mitgliederversammlung) documents
- Participation tools (polls, discussion forums, working groups)

### Phase 5: Design Principles for NGOs

**1. Trust over polish**

A hyper-polished corporate site can actually work against an NGO. Visitors may wonder:
"Where did they get the money for this website?" Design should be clean and professional,
but not flashy. Credibility comes from content and transparency, not from parallax effects.

That said — amateur design also kills trust. The sweet spot is: professional, clear, warm,
and human. Not corporate, not scrappy.

**2. Accessibility is non-negotiable**

NGOs exist to serve the public. If your site excludes people with disabilities, you're
contradicting your mission. Build to WCAG 2.1 AA minimum:

- Color contrast ratios: 4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text
- Full keyboard navigability
- Semantic HTML (proper heading hierarchy, landmarks, alt text)
- Responsive design that works on all devices
- Consider Leichte Sprache (easy language) versions for key pages
- Consider Deutsche Gebärdensprache (DGS) videos for core content

**3. Mobile-first, low-bandwidth aware**

Many supporters will encounter the site via social media links on their phone. The mobile
experience is the primary experience. Additionally, consider that campaign moments (protests,
events) mean people are on mobile with potentially poor connectivity. Keep pages lightweight.

**4. Action-oriented, not information-heavy**

Every page should have a clear "what do you want me to do?" moment. Don't just inform —
mobilize. But balance this: never make the visitor feel manipulated. Urgency is fine;
manufactured panic is not. NGOs live and die on trust.

**5. Bilingual / multilingual awareness**

Many German NGOs work internationally or serve multilingual communities. Plan for i18n from
the start even if launching in one language. Use proper `lang` attributes, consider RTL support
if relevant, and structure content so translation is additive, not a rebuild.

**6. Visual language**

- Use real photography of real people and events. Stock photos of "diverse group smiling at
  camera" destroy credibility instantly. If you don't have photography, use illustration,
  iconography, or strong typography — never fake stock.
- Colors should reflect the organization's identity, not trends. Political associations often
  have established brand colors — use them consistently.
- Typography should be highly readable. This is not the place for experimental display fonts
  in body copy. Use a clean, accessible body font and a distinctive but legible heading font.
  Ensure the font supports German characters (umlauts, ß) properly.

### Phase 6: Technical Architecture

**Recommended stack for most NGOs:**

NGOs typically need:
- Low maintenance overhead (volunteer-run, limited tech capacity)
- Easy content updates by non-technical staff
- Low hosting costs
- Good SEO
- Reliability (site must be up during campaign peaks)

**Good options:**
- Static site generators (Astro, Hugo, Next.js static) + headless CMS (Decap, Strapi, Sanity)
  → Best performance, lowest cost, most secure
- WordPress with careful setup → Familiar to non-technical editors, huge ecosystem, but
  requires security maintenance
- Website builders (Webflow, Squarespace) → Fast to launch, limited customization

**Must-have integrations:**
- Donation processing: Stripe, PayPal, or specialized (Betterplace, Fundraisingbox, GoFundMe)
- Newsletter: Mailchimp, Sendinblue, CleverReach (check DSGVO compliance, prefer EU-hosted)
- Analytics: Matomo self-hosted or Plausible (privacy-friendly, DSGVO-compliant without consent
  if configured correctly)
- Forms: Self-hosted or DSGVO-compliant (not Google Forms for sensitive data)
- Social sharing: Shariff or similar privacy-friendly sharing buttons

**Avoid:**
- Google Analytics (requires cookie consent, US data transfer issues under DSGVO)
- Facebook Pixel (same issues, plus reputational risk for political orgs)
- Heavy JavaScript frameworks for content-first sites (SPAs are rarely appropriate for NGOs)
- Any tracking that could be seen as surveillance by the org's constituency

### Phase 7: Donation and Membership UX

This is where NGO web design has its own specialized UX discipline.

**Donation page principles:**
- Show impact: "€25 funds one workshop" is more compelling than "Donate €25"
- Provide tiers: suggest 3-4 amounts with impact descriptions, plus custom amount
- Recurring > one-time: make monthly giving the default, one-time the alternative
- Reduce friction: minimal form fields, multiple payment methods
- Show trust signals: Gemeinnützigkeit status, what % goes to programs vs. admin
- Thank immediately: confirmation page + email with Zuwendungsbescheinigung info

**Membership signup:**
- Explain what membership means: rights (voting at Mitgliederversammlung), benefits,
  contribution amounts
- Make the Satzung accessible (link to it, don't hide it)
- Beitragsstaffelung: offer income-based tiers if possible (Sozialbeitrag, Normalbeitrag,
  Förderbeitrag)
- Digital-first onboarding: welcome email sequence, link to member resources

## Implementation Checklist

Before launch, verify:

**Legal compliance:**
- [ ] Impressum complete and accessible from every page
- [ ] Datenschutzerklärung covers all data processing
- [ ] Cookie consent implemented correctly
- [ ] Accessibility statement published
- [ ] Satzung publicly available

**Accessibility:**
- [ ] WCAG 2.1 AA baseline met
- [ ] Keyboard navigation works throughout
- [ ] Screen reader tested (VoiceOver / NVDA)
- [ ] Color contrast checked
- [ ] Alt text on all meaningful images
- [ ] Proper heading hierarchy (h1 → h2 → h3, no skips)
- [ ] Focus indicators visible

**Performance:**
- [ ] Mobile page load under 3 seconds on 3G
- [ ] Images optimized (WebP with fallback)
- [ ] No render-blocking resources
- [ ] Core Web Vitals passing

**Content:**
- [ ] Mission clearly stated within 5 seconds of landing
- [ ] Primary CTA visible without scrolling
- [ ] Team / board page with real photos
- [ ] At least one transparency element (report, finances, Satzung)
- [ ] Press contact easily findable

**Functionality:**
- [ ] Donation flow tested end-to-end
- [ ] Newsletter signup works and sends confirmation (double opt-in required in Germany)
- [ ] Contact form delivers to correct inbox
- [ ] Social sharing generates correct OG meta tags
- [ ] RSS feed for news/blog (journalists use these)

## Common Mistakes in NGO Web Design

**"Let's make it look like a startup"**
NGOs are not startups. Gradient CTAs, growth metrics dashboards, and "disruptive" language
undermine the trust and seriousness that civil society organizations need to project.

**"Everything is equally important"**
The homepage tries to cover every campaign, every event, every position. Result: nothing
stands out. Pick 1-2 hero priorities and let the rest live in navigation.

**"We'll add accessibility later"**
Accessibility retrofitting costs 3-5x more than building it in from the start. Plan for it
in Phase 1 or accept that you'll exclude a significant portion of your potential constituency.

**"Members can just call us"**
If existing members can't find internal documents, event dates, and participation tools on
the website, engagement drops. The member area doesn't need to be complex — but it needs
to exist.

**"We don't need analytics, we're not commercial"**
Understanding which campaigns drive traffic, which pages people leave from, and where
donation drop-off happens is as important for NGOs as for businesses. Use privacy-friendly
analytics, but use analytics.

**"Our developer will handle the Impressum"**
Legal compliance is not a developer task. Have it reviewed by someone who understands TMG,
DSGVO, and Vereinsrecht. The developer implements it; they don't draft it.
